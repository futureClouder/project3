The article I chose was published by John A NaslundKelly A AschbrennerGregory J McHugoJürgen UnützerLisa A MarschStephen J Bartels in 2019 in Early Intervention in Psychiatry. the article titled " Exploring opportunities to support mental health care using social media: A survey of social media users with mental illness". This article is about the relevant psychological research. The researchers recruited volunteers using Twitter, which is a mainstream social networking platform. The authors chose Twitter for this study because most of the users and the content they shared were publicly accessible, so that the researchers could know if the selected subjects were potentially mentally ill. Researchers used a questionnaire format to conduct the survey. The group of volunteers came from the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, Malaysia and other countries, with more than half of the volunteers coming from the United States. The volunteers were of a wide range of ages and had a high level of education overall. Participants' self-reported mental illness diagnoses did not differ between age groups. More than two-thirds of the sample reported a diagnosis of serious mental illness, mostly schizophrenia spectrum disorder; bipolar disorder or major depressive disorder. More than half of the participants reported being hospitalized at least once for a mental illness, and most reported experiencing moderate to very severe mental health symptoms that interfered with their daily activities. Through the survey, the researchers also found that these people enjoyed using social media such as Twitter, ins, and snapchat, and were interested in using social media for psychotherapy. Through their analysis, the researchers concluded that using social media to help people with mental illness would cover more age groups and apply to more people, but not to the elderly population, who are less likely to publicly disclose their mental illness online and have little access to these social media platforms. The study also shed light on the importance of social media for people with mental illness to access peer support. Researchers found that two-thirds of participants reported using social media to connect with others who have a mental illness, and more than 70 percent of participants used social media in this way at least once a week. More than half of the participants also reported using social media to share personal experiences about living with mental illness and to learn from others. According to previous studies, access to illness experiences shared by other patients in online communities may be valuable in supporting illness self-management.
